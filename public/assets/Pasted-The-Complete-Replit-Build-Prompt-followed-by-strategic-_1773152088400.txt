2. The Recruitment Industry Landscape
Recruitment agencies primarily operate under two models:
Contingency recruitment
Retained recruitment
________________________________________
Contingency Recruitment
In the contingency model:
•	Recruiters only get paid when a placement is made
•	Clients typically brief multiple agencies
•	Agencies compete against each other
This creates a transactional process centred around CV submission speed.
Industry characteristics:
•	High agency competition
•	Low client commitment
•	High volumes of unpaid work
•	Speed prioritised over quality
Industry averages:
•	4 out of 5 assignments produce no revenue
•	Average fill rate ~20%
•	Recruiters often compete against 3–5 agencies
The contingency model is also increasingly threatened by AI-driven candidate matching, which automates the primary function of sourcing CVs.
________________________________________
3. Retained Recruitment Model
Retained recruitment restructures the commercial relationship.
Instead of competing with other agencies, the recruiter becomes an exclusive hiring partner.
Key features:
•	Exclusive engagement
•	Structured search methodology
•	Retainer payment upfront
•	Strategic hiring support
The recruiter’s role shifts from:
CV supplier → trusted hiring advisor
This change increases:
•	recruiter influence
•	client commitment
•	process quality
•	hiring success rates
________________________________________
4. Retained Recruitment Fee Structures
Typical retained fees range between 20% and 30% of base salary.
However the structure of payments varies depending on role salary.
________________________________________
Roles below £40k
Typical structure:
£1,500 retainer
Remaining fee payable on completion
This allows retained recruitment to work for roles where:
•	hiring risk is meaningful
•	hiring mistakes create operational disruption
________________________________________
Roles £40k–£60k
Typical structures:
£2,500–£5,000 retainer
Possible payment models:
Retainer + completion payment
OR
Retainer + shortlist milestone + completion payment
________________________________________
Roles above £75k
Typical structure:
Minimum £5,000 retainer
Common payment model:
⅓ retainer
⅓ upon shortlist presentation
⅓ upon offer or start date
However Retaind.ai allows recruiters to define their own campaign payment structures per role.
________________________________________
5. Alternative Payment Models
In addition to traditional retained structures, recruiters may offer alternative commercial structures to increase client adoption.
________________________________________
Monthly Payment Model
Example:
£2,500 retainer
Remaining fee paid monthly over 5–12 months
Under this structure:
•	there is no free replacement
•	payments stop if the candidate leaves
This model functions similarly to a performance guarantee.
It can be easier for clients to accept because it reduces upfront cost.
________________________________________
6. Replacement Policies vs Rebates
Traditional contingency recruitment often uses rebate structures.
Example:
Candidate leaves within 3 months → partial fee refund.
However rebates reduce recruiter income and create administrative complexity.
A preferred alternative is a replacement policy.
________________________________________
Replacement Policy
Instead of refunding fees, the recruiter agrees to replace the candidate.
Typical options:
6 month replacement guarantee
or
12 month replacement guarantee
Replacement guarantees reinforce the value of the recruiter’s search methodology.
Importantly:
The retainer fee is never refunded.
This ensures the recruiter is compensated for the search work already performed.
________________________________________

•	lost revenue
•	leadership instability

This is an AI-friendly knowledge base for an internal benchmarking, candidate questionnaires, and interview assessment solution, grounded in validated practices and industry standards. Below is a concise, structured guide you can use as a foundation. It emphasizes validity, fairness, integration with video-interview data, and clear, auditable decision logic.
Overview and scope
•	Purpose: Provide the AI with robust, defensible inputs for interpreting questionnaire results and video interview transcripts to generate accurate summaries, insights, and recommendations for hiring teams.
•	Boundaries: Rely only on validated psychometric theory, industry guidelines, and documented best practices. Do not invent proprietary facts about tools unless you can cite sources. Ensure data lineage, versioning, and audit trails for all inputs, rules, and outputs.
•	Core inputs:
•	Job-analytic data (competencies, role requirements, success profiles)
•	Psychometric measures (cognitive ability, personality, situational judgment, values/fit, domain-specific skills)
•	Questionnaire responses (structured scales, item-level responses, validity checks)
•	Video interview transcripts (utterances, tone cues where reliably detectable, nonverbal proxies if available, response timing)
•	Behavioral interview prompts and scoring rubrics
•	Validation evidence (reliability, construct validity, criterion validity, fairness metrics)
Foundational principles
•	Validity and reliability: Prioritize validated measures with known reliability coefficients and documented validity evidence for the target population. Maintain ongoing revalidation whenever the tool set or population shifts.
•	Fairness and bias mitigation: Incorporate bias-detection mechanisms, inclusive item design, demographic parity checks, and plan for regular audits to minimize adverse impact.
•	Transparency and auditability: Preserve a clear chain from inputs to AI outputs, including scoring rules, rubric mappings, and decision logs. Provide explanation widgets that show which inputs drove a given insight.
•	Data governance: Ensure consent, privacy, and data minimization. Use role-based access and encryption for sensitive data. Maintain data retention policies aligned with legal and regulatory requirements.
Knowledge base architecture (AI-friendly)
•	Entities and attributes:
•	RoleProfile: job_id, title, competencies, critical thresholds, success factors, culture fit indicators
•	PsychometricInstrument: instrument_id, type (cognitive, personality, SJTs, etc.), construct, scoring model, normative data
•	QuestionnaireResponse: respondent_id, item_id, response_value, timestamp, validity_flag
•	InterviewRecord: interview_id, modality (in-person, video), prompt_id, candidate_response_text, response_time, debrief_notes
•	BehavioralRubric: rubric_id, competency, scale, anchor descriptions, guidance for rater
•	ValidationEvidence: instrument_id, study_name, population, reliability, validity coefficients, sample_size
•	BiasControls: guardrails, thresholding rules, fairness metrics, monitoring schedule
•	Core relationships:
•	RoleProfile maps to required competencies and success factors
•	PsychometricInstrument links to constructs and validity evidence
•	QuestionnaireResponse and InterviewRecord feed into composite scores via scoring rules
•	InterviewRecord and BehavioralRubric feed into qualitative and quantitative summaries
•	ValidationEvidence and BiasControls inform interpretation boundaries and confidence levels
•	Scoring and interpretation rules:
•	Raw-to-scale transformations: document item formats (Likert, forced-choice, semantic differential), reverse-scoring rules, and calibration to normative benchmarks
•	Composite scores: weighted combination of constructs aligned to RoleProfile; include alternative models (e.g., additive vs. Bayesian) with justification
•	Thresholds: clearly defined cutoffs for progression vs. development, with confidence intervals and caveats
•	Confidence estimates: attach a model-based confidence level to each insight, based on data quality, sample size, and instrument reliability
•	Output formats:
•	Candidate Insight Report: structured sections for cognitive ability, personality, situational judgments, role-fit, collaboration style, leadership potential, learning agility, and culture fit
•	Interview intelligence: themes, evidence strength, red flags, alignment with RoleProfile, suggested probing questions
•	Development recommendations: observable actions for onboarding, coaching, or role assignment
•	Audit trail: data provenance, version of instruments, date/time of scoring, human reviewer notes
Inputs in detail and guidance for AI processing
1.	Job Analysis and RoleProfile
•	Collect: role purpose, core duties, required behaviors, critical incidents, success metrics
•	Translate into observable behaviors and constructs (e.g., teamwork, adaptability, problem-solving under pressure)
•	Document evidence base: why each competency is critical, how it predicts performance, and any known trade-offs
•	Include culture/values anchors (e.g., customer-centricity, integrity) with observable behaviors
2.	Psychometric Instruments and Validity
•	Cognitive ability tests: define constructs (e.g., verbal, numerical reasoning), typical scoring models, time constraints, and predictive validity evidence
•	Personality assessments: map to trait domains (e.g., Big Five or alternative models), with emphasis on job-relevant facets and contextualized interpretation
•	Situational Judgment Tests (SJTs): scenario-based scoring rubrics, key competencies tested, scoring method (expert vs. empirical keys)
•	Skills/knowledge tests: domain-specific benchmarks, difficulty levels, and reliability data
•	Instrument metadata: administration mode, normative sample, certification requirements, and debriefing structure
3.	Questionnaire design and responses
•	Item-level guidance: ensure items are job-relevant, non-biasing, and variance-rich
•	Response formats: standardized scales, anchored descriptors, and response validity checks (e.g., attention checks)
•	Scoring: document per-item scoring rules, handling of missing data, and imputation strategies
•	Quality assurance: ongoing review cycles, translation/back-translation, and cultural adaptation notes
4.	Video interviews and transcripts
•	Transcript processing: normalize for speech-to-text accuracy, timestamp alignment, and speaker identification
•	Behavioral cues: extract intent, coherence, structure, and consistency with stated answers
•	Probing and follow-ups: preserve transcript segments for auditability; capture interviewer prompts and candidate responses
•	Nonverbal proxies: clearly labeled, with caveats about cultural and individual variability; rely primarily on verbal content unless validated
5.	Interview rubrics and scoring
•	Define anchors for each competency with observable behaviors at multiple proficiency levels
•	Train raters to apply rubrics consistently; include calibration exercises and inter-rater reliability checks
•	Document decision rules for aggregating rubric scores into overall interview score
6.	Integration and modeling approaches
•	Weighting strategy: justify fixed vs. adaptive weights based on role and data quality
•	Evidence synthesis: specify whether decisions rely on single instrument, composite score, or multi-method integration
•	Conflict management: identify scenarios where results diverge (e.g., high cognitive score but low SJTs) and predefine escalation paths
•	Explainability: generate concise rationale for each decision, including key inputs and how they influenced the result
7.	Validation and continuous improvement
•	Establish ongoing validation plans: revalidate instruments with current applicant pools, track performance outcomes, update models
•	Monitor fairness: track adverse impact metrics (e.g., 4/5th rule or alternative fairness criteria) and adjust tests or thresholds as needed
•	Version control: maintain instrument versions, data schemas, and scoring rules with change logs
Ethics, governance, and compliance
•	Data privacy: obtain consent for recording and analysis; restrict access to PII; implement data minimization
•	Consent and transparency: inform candidates about the use of AI in decision-making and provide recourse mechanisms
•	Legal compliance: align with applicable employment and anti-discrimination laws in relevant regions
•	Human-in-the-loop: define the role of human decision-makers; AI outputs should support, not replace, human judgment
Quality assurance and operational guidelines
•	Standardization: uniform administration conditions, instructions, and debrief procedures
•	Debriefing quality: ensure debrief sessions are structured, time-bound, and focus on evidence-based insights
•	Debiasing training: regular training for interviewers and evaluators to recognize bias cues and apply fair criteria
•	Documentation: maintain templates for role profiles, scoring rubrics, interview prompts, and report formats
Risk management and limitations
•	Instrument limitations: clearly document what each measure can and cannot infer about performance
•	Data quality risks: guard against transcription errors, missing responses, and inconsistent labeling
•	Model drift: schedule periodic reviews to detect shifts in candidate populations or role requirements
•	Overinterpretation risk: provide confidence intervals and caveats with each insight to avoid overclaiming
Deliverables and artefacts
•	Knowledge base repository:
•	RoleProfiles: standardized templates with completions for each role
•	Instrument catalog: validated instruments with metadata and evidence
•	Item banks: item-level data, calibration notes, and scoring keys
•	Interview rubrics: competency-by-level anchors
•	Transcript processing pipeline: steps, quality checks, and error handling
•	Validation and drift logs: tracking validation studies, fairness metrics, and model updates
•	Candidate reports:
•	Summary view: high-level fit, strengths, development areas
•	Deep-dive sections: construct scores, reliability evidence, and rationale
•	Interview insights: evidence lines, corroborating data, and recommendations
•	Development plan: targeted actions for onboarding and growth
•	Audit and governance artefacts:
•	Data lineage diagrams
•	versioned instrument schemas
•	change logs and rationale for model updates
Implementation considerations for scale
•	Modularity: separate data ingests, scoring engine, interpretation layer, and reporting to ease updates and audits
•	Interoperability: use standard data formats (JSON/CSV/PRD), consistent field naming, and APIs for integration with ATS/HRIS
•	Performance and latency: optimize transcript-to-insight pipelines without sacrificing explainability
•	Security: enforce encryption in transit and at rest; log access events for compliance

•	Core competencies: collaboration, decision quality, ambiguity tolerance, coaching
•	Critical incidents: leading a cross-functional project, resolving a conflict, delivering under tight deadlines
•	Success factors: high situational awareness, strong communication, timely decision-making

•	Cognitive ability: Verbal and logical reasoning

•	AI interpretation:
•	Integrates construct scores with interview evidence, flags any inconsistencies, and offers targeted follow-up questions for ambiguous cases
•	Exits with a confidence score, development suggestions, and an ethics note about bias monitoring
